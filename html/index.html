Where should Philly's next park be?

Why does Philly need this project?

Project Proposal

As the COVID-19 stretched into end of 2020, people in Philadelphia began to flock to outdoor green spaces in and around the city. A report from the Trust for Public Land finds that although green spaces in Philadelphia is accessible to most citizens, the amont of green spaces varies greatly based on income and race.

And according to Inquirer, some Philly neighborhoods can be up to 20°F (6.7°C) hotter than others during the summer months due to Urban Heat Island Effect. The drainage problem of the flood has also become a worrying problem in Philadelphia.

Moreover, Philadelphia Parks and Recreation budget was cut by 20% during the last fiscal year, equating to about $12.5 million, which means that the budget on new green spaces is limited.!

Green Space Equality

Philadelphia Green Space Inequality by Race

The equity of Philadelphia's green space is an issue worth exploring. We built a two-dimensional choropleth map to show the relationship between the park area of different census tracts and their race condition, where the park area is the total area of the park that can be reached within 500 meters of the census tracts. The upper right corner of the legend is the smallest park area and percentage of white and the bottom left is the opposite. So the dark areas in the map are where we need to focus. Philadelphia's next park may be born in these locations. The scatter plot of park area vs. race shows a roughly upward linear relationship, indicating that there is indeed a fairness problem in the distribution of parks in Philadelphia.
