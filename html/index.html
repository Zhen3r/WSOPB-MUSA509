Where should Philly's next park be?

Why does Philly need this project?

Project Proposal

As the COVID-19 stretched into end of 2020, people in Philadelphia began to flock to outdoor green spaces in and around the city. A report from the Trust for Public Land finds that although green spaces in Philadelphia is accessible to most citizens, the amont of green spaces varies greatly based on income and race.

And according to Inquirer, some Philly neighborhoods can be up to 20°F (6.7°C) hotter than others during the summer months due to Urban Heat Island Effect. The drainage problem of the flood has also become a worrying problem in Philadelphia.

Moreover, Philadelphia Parks and Recreation budget was cut by 20% during the last fiscal year, equating to about $12.5 million, which means that the budget on new green spaces is limited.!

Green Space Equality

Philadelphia Green Space Inequality by Race

The equity of Philadelphia's green space is an issue worth exploring. We built a two-dimensional choropleth map to show the relationship between the park area of different census tracts and their race condition, where the park area is the total area of the park that can be reached within 500 meters of the census tracts. The upper right corner of the legend is the smallest park area and percentage of white and the bottom left is the opposite. So the dark areas in the map are where we need to focus. Philadelphia's next park may be born in these locations. The scatter plot of park area vs. race shows a roughly upward linear relationship, indicating that there is indeed a fairness problem in the distribution of parks in Philadelphia.

Green Space Ecology

Philadelphia Urban Heat Island

Parks and green spaces can also play a great role in improving the environment. The red area in the picture is where the temperature in Philadelphia is high, which is where the urban heat island effect is significant. Increasing the construction of parks in these areas can effectively alleviate the temperature imbalance.

Green Space Ecology

Philadelphia Flood Plain

Flooding from storm surges and heavy rainfall can be one of the most devastating effects of storms — and scientists say climate change is making those storms worse. Storm-resistant green spaces can help cities defend themselves against flooding. The purple area on the right is the once-in-100-year flood plain, and the blue area is the once-in-500-year flood plain. These areas are where parks are needed to prevent flooding and reduce the damage of it.

Green Space Economy

Costs to build a park

To figure out the cost to build a new park on a certain location in Philly, a map of cost by census tracts was calculated based on land use, land value and building volume. In terms of land use, we set the index of cost of Residential, Commercial, Industrial, Civic/Institution, Transportation areas as 1, Water area as 0.75, Culture/Recreation area as 0.5 and Park/Open Space and Vacant or other usage as 0.25, because the renovation cost of built environment is undoubtedly huge, while vacant lots will be less expensive. For the cost of land value and demolition, we directly divide the corresponding value and building volumes into five quantiles. And the total cost is the average of three costs mentioned above. As in shown in the map, the cost of the city center is the largest, which is in line with our expectations.
